2  Related Work

IRI prediction models fall broadly into three generations. The first generation, exemplified by the AASHO Road Test serviceability equations, [1]’s World Bank incremental deterioration models (later folded into HDM-III/HDM-4), and the M-E Pavement Design Guide’s smoothness models [2], [3], uses linear or mildly non-linear regression on a small, hand-selected set of distress and site variables. [3]’s companion appendix to the one this paper builds on — Appendix OO-1, for flexible rather than rigid pavements — established the same distress-based regression form for AC pavements that Appendix PP later established for JPCP/CRCP, and both remain the operational core of AASHTOWare Pavement ME today. Numerous follow-on studies refit this linear form to different regions and pavement types: [4] and [5] examined how individual distress types (cracking, rutting, patching) trade off against each other in explaining IRI on the AASHO and WesTrack test sections respectively; [6] and [7] fit comparable regressions specifically for HMA-overlaid and two-lane flexible pavements. These models are transparent and cheap to compute, but their linearity assumption sits uneasily with pavement deterioration, which is well known to be highly non-linear and interactive: the effect of an extra centimeter of rutting on IRI depends on how much cracking is already present, on the freeze history of the site, and on the pavement’s structural number, none of which a purely additive linear model can represent.

The second generation replaced the linear form with flexible learners trained directly on LTPP or state DOT pavement management data. Early work leaned on artificial neural networks: [8]’s back-propagation network and [9]’s ANN/GMDH comparison both showed sizeable accuracy gains over linear regression on flexible-pavement IRI, and [10] benchmarked statistical and AI-based remaining-service-life models side by side on flexible and composite pavements. [11]’s comparative framework for empirical pavement performance models was among the first to argue systematically that model form, not just coefficients, should be selected empirically rather than assumed. Tree ensembles arrived next: [12]’s random forest, applied to LTPP flexible pavements by [13] with a reported testing-set \(R^2\) of 0.974, and the gradient boosting family popularized by tools like [14]’s LightGBM and applied to asphalt-overlay performance by [15], consistently outperformed linear M-E benchmarks in the literature on raw prediction accuracy, at the cost of interpretability: a 400-tree ensemble does not hand an engineer a coefficient to reason about.

The third generation, which [16]’s paper and this one both belong to, restores interpretability without giving up the ensemble’s accuracy advantage, using SHapley Additive exPlanations [17]. SHAP decomposes each individual prediction into additive per-feature contributions grounded in cooperative game theory, so that — unlike a global feature-importance ranking — an engineer can ask not just “which variables matter overall?” but “why did the model predict this section will roughen this fast?” [16] used SHAP to show that a simplified six-feature ThunderGBM model retained most of the accuracy of the full twenty-feature version, a genuinely useful finding for agencies with limited data-collection budgets.

What none of these studies do — flexible-pavement or otherwise — is (a) apply the same explainable-ensemble recipe to rigid pavements and compare the two families side by side, (b) directly incorporate Falling Weight Deflectometer joint load-transfer efficiency — the mechanistic quantity that faulting is itself a downstream symptom of — as a learned feature rather than leaving it implicit, or (c) report accuracy under a splitting strategy that accounts for LTPP’s repeated-visit panel structure. Those three gaps are what this work addresses.
