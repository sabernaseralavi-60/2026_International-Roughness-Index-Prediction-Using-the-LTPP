4.1 Dataset summary

Table 4.1 summarizes the two assembled panels.

Both panels span a comparable range of in-service roughness (Figure 4.1), with the familiar right-skew of a well-maintained highway network: most sections cluster between 1 and 2 m/km, with a thin tail of rougher outliers that any predictive model has to work hard to capture.

Age and IRI show the expected — and expectedly noisy — relationship (Figure 4.2): both families roughen somewhat with age, but the relationship is weak on its own, which is precisely the motivation for a multivariate model that can weigh age against structure, climate, and accumulated distress simultaneously rather than reading it off a single scatterplot.

4.2 Model comparison

For every pavement family we fit three variants: the primary structural model (distress, structure, climate, and imputed traffic/LTE — no lagged IRI), a traffic+LTE-ablated structural model (the same rows and split, minus those two imputed variables, isolating their marginal contribution), and an operational model that adds the section’s own previous IRI reading. Table 4.2 reports held-out LightGBM performance across all of them; Table 4.3 and Table 4.4 give the full four-model comparison (linear, random forest, Optuna-tuned ANN, Optuna-tuned LightGBM) for the primary structural variant.

Four findings emerge from Table 4.2. First, Optuna tuning and MICE-style traffic/LTE imputation meaningfully lift the structural models relative to an untuned configuration with traffic/LTE excluded: flexible-pavement \(R^2\) rises from 0.333 to 0.414, and pooled rigid from 0.254 to 0.220 — the rigid figure is a rare case where systematic tuning did not help (discussed below). Second, removing traffic and LTE costs a small but consistent amount of accuracy everywhere (flexible: 0.414 → 0.395; pooled rigid: 0.220 → 0.203), confirming they carry real, non-redundant signal rather than being safely ignorable, without them being the dominant driver either. Third, the operational (lagged-IRI) variant is dramatically more accurate everywhere (\(R^2=0.698\) flexible, \(0.391\) pooled rigid), which is expected — IRI is strongly autocorrelated within a section — and is reported as a distinct, clearly-labeled model rather than folded into the “structural” headline number, since the two answer different engineering questions (see Chapter 3). Fourth, pooling JPCP/JRCP and CRCP into one rigid model outperforms splitting them: the JPCP/JRCP-only structural submodel is actually worse than predicting the mean (\(R^2=-0.294\), \(n=118\) sections), and the CRCP-only submodel, while positive (\(R^2=0.226\)), carries enormous cross-validation variance (\(\pm0.246\)) from having only 61 sections to work with. Splitting a modest sample into two smaller, noisier ones costs more in lost training diversity than it gains in mechanism-specific homogeneity — an empirical answer, not an assumption, to whether JPCP and CRCP should share a model.

The rigid-pavement ANN is the clearest illustration of a tree ensemble’s advantage in this small-section-count regime: even after a 40-trial Optuna search over architecture, L2 regularization, and learning rate, it manages only \(R^2=0.009\) (pooled) to \(R^2=-1.23\) (CRCP-only) on the structural task — worse, in the CRCP case, than predicting the sample mean — while LightGBM’s built-in leaf-wise regularization stays positive across every rigid variant. Because the ANN is tuned against the same group-aware cross-validation objective as LightGBM, its weaker performance reflects a genuine bias-variance trade-off in a few-hundred-section regime rather than inadequate tuning of the baseline.

NoteWhy the ensemble wins on speed, not just accuracy

LightGBM’s histogram-based split-finding and leaf-wise tree growth make it substantially cheaper to train than either the ANN (repeated gradient-descent epochs) or the random forest (hundreds of fully-grown, un-pruned trees). Across every family and variant in Table 4.2, tuned LightGBM trains 1.7\(\times\) to 9\(\times\) faster than the random forest baseline. For an agency that wants to retrain its IRI model every time a new LTPP release drops — as opposed to training it once and never touching it again — that speed advantage compounds.

4.3 Robustness and sensitivity checks

Five further checks accompany the headline structural model, addressing gradient-boosting implementation choice, split methodology, age provenance, imputation method, and candidate feature additions in turn.

Gradient-boosting implementation. Table 4.5 compares LightGBM against XGBoost [1] and CatBoost [2], each independently tuned with a 25-trial Optuna search against the same group-aware cross-validation objective as the headline model.

LightGBM is the best- or near-best-performing implementation in three of four cases, and is within 0.03-0.06 \(R^2\) of the best alternative in the fourth (JPCP/JRCP-only, where CatBoost edges ahead in an already low-accuracy regime). The choice among modern gradient-boosting implementations does not change any of this study’s conclusions.

Split methodology, and why its effect varies by family. Table 4.6 reports, for each family, the same Optuna-tuned hyperparameters evaluated under a naive row-level split and under the group-aware split used throughout this study, isolating the contribution of splitting strategy alone from any confound with hyperparameter selection.

The inflation from a naive split is not constant: it is negligible for flexible pavements (0.417 vs. 0.414) but substantial for rigid pavements, and largest of all for the JPCP/JRCP-only submodel (0.355 vs. -0.294, an inflation of 0.649 \(R^2\)) — precisely the configuration whose structural feature set explains the least genuine variance. This is consistent with a specific mechanism rather than a generic warning: when a naive split allows repeated visits of the same section into both training and test data, the model can recover a section’s persistent, unmeasured characteristics from its training-set visits and apply that section-specific signature to held-out visits of the same section. That recovery is most profitable, and therefore produces the largest apparent accuracy gain, exactly where the measured covariates explain the smallest share of true variance, since more of the outcome is then attributable to persistent section identity that only a naive split allows to leak through. A well-tuned model evaluated on a well-explained outcome (flexible pavements) has comparatively little unexplained section-specific variance left for a naive split to exploit; a model facing a poorly-explained outcome (JPCP/JRCP structural roughness) has a great deal, and a naive split rewards memorizing it. This is a more specific and testable claim than the coarser observation that an early, less-regularized configuration of the flexible-pavement model showed a large naive-vs-grouped gap (0.52 vs. 0.33): leakage-driven inflation should scale with the fraction of outcome variance the structural features fail to explain, a pattern Table 4.6 confirms across four independently-tuned models spanning a wide range of explanatory power.

Age provenance, imputation method, and candidate feature additions. Table 4.7 summarizes three further checks against the headline structural model.

Restricting to records with a verified INV_AGE.CONSTRUCTION_DATE (excluding the age-proxy fallback) leaves only 19-61 sections per family — too few for a stable held-out estimate, and the resulting \(R^2\) values swing widely, including strongly negative, in both directions relative to the full-sample headline figure. We read this as a sample-size artifact of the restriction itself rather than evidence that the age proxy is unreliable: with a test set of 4-12 sections, a handful of atypical records can dominate \(R^2\). A dedicated larger sample with universal construction-date coverage would be needed to test the age proxy’s effect directly. Multiple imputation, in contrast, gives a clear and reassuring answer: a five-draw stochastic multiple-imputation ensemble produces held-out \(R^2\) within 0.003-0.05 of the single-imputation pipeline used throughout, for every family, with the largest discrepancy (CRCP-only, \(\pm0.038\) SD) occurring in the family with the fewest sections. The single-pass MICE approach is not a material source of uncertainty relative to sampling variability at these sample sizes. Finally, adding a binary base-type indicator (granular vs. treated/stabilized base) and subgrade plasticity index to the structural feature set reduces held-out \(R^2\) in every family (e.g., flexible: 0.414 → 0.346), rather than improving it. Plasticity index is populated for only 17-18% of records nationally, and imputing a variable at that missingness level appears to introduce more noise than signal once passed through the same training-fold-only imputer used for traffic and LTE; the binary base-type indicator, while fully observed, does not compensate. We report this as a genuine negative result: at least for these two candidate variables under this imputation strategy, expanding the structural feature set beyond the headline set does not improve on it.

4.4 What drives IRI: SHAP feature attribution

Figure 4.3 and Figure 4.4 show SHAP summary plots for the trained structural ensemble models. Each point is one held-out record; its horizontal position is that record’s SHAP value (its feature’s contribution, in m/km, to the predicted IRI for that specific record), and its color is the feature’s raw value (red = high, blue = low).

For flexible pavements, MEPDG transverse crack length is the single strongest predictor, followed by patching area and mean annual temperature — a data-driven confirmation of exactly the distress hierarchy that [3]’s hand-fit equation assumed by design, plus a climate interaction (temperature) that the fixed-form linear model cannot represent on its own. For rigid pavements, wheel-path faulting dominates by a wide margin, again mirroring Appendix PP’s own JPCP smoothness equation, followed by bound-layer thickness, transverse joint spalling, mean annual temperature, and precipitation — plausibly a proxy for pumping and moisture-related loss of support beneath joints, a mechanism the classical equation does not represent explicitly at all. FWD load-transfer efficiency ranks 10th of 14 features: a real, non-trivial contribution (consistent with the ablation result earlier in this chapter) rather than a dominant one, with low LTE (poor joint performance) pushing predicted IRI up, exactly the mechanistic pathway (loss of aggregate interlock or dowel efficiency → faulting → roughness) that Appendix PP’s equation encodes only implicitly through the faulting term alone. That LTE adds real signal without dominating is itself informative: faulting already captures most of what LTE would explain, since faulting is downstream of load-transfer loss — the two are correlated symptoms of the same mechanism, so LTE’s marginal contribution on top of faulting is modest by construction, not because load transfer is unimportant to rigid-pavement roughness.

The two climate variables warrant a more mechanistic reading than “climate matters.” For flexible pavements, mean annual temperature interacts with cracking rather than substituting for it in the SHAP plot (Figure 4.3): its effect is concentrated among records that already carry non-trivial transverse crack length, consistent with the well-established sensitivity of asphalt binder stiffness to temperature — a colder climate embrittles the binder and accelerates thermal (transverse) cracking for a given traffic and structural exposure, so temperature acts as an effect modifier on the crack-length–IRI relationship rather than an independent driver. For rigid pavements, precipitation’s contribution is concentrated among high-faulting records, which is the pattern implied by the classical erosion mechanism underlying pumping: water that infiltrates through joints or shoulders erodes fines from the base or subgrade, and repeated heavy axle loads pump the resulting slurry out from under the slab, progressively reducing support directly beneath the joint and increasing differential deflection across it. This is precisely the moisture-related loss-of-support mechanism that the M-E site factor \(SF = \text{Age} \times (1+FI) \times (1+P_{200})/10^6\) [3] was constructed to proxy in aggregate rather than represent as a direct interaction with faulting; the SHAP-based model recovers the interaction directly from the joint distribution of precipitation and faulting rather than through a pre-specified multiplicative form.

Notably, the model recovers this structure without being given the M-E equation’s functional form in advance — it is discovered from the joint distribution of distress, climate, LTE, and IRI across hundreds of LTPP sections. This agreement with established mechanistic-empirical relationships is a useful check on the SHAP explanation itself: a data-driven attribution that recovers known pavement physics is more credible than one that cannot be reconciled with prior engineering knowledge.

advance** — it is discovered from the joint distribution of distress, climate, LTE, and IRI across hundreds of
LTPP sections. This agreement with established mechanistic-empirical relationships is a useful check on the
SHAP explanation itself: a data-driven attribution that recovers known pavement physics is more credible than
one that cannot be reconciled with prior engineering knowledge.
